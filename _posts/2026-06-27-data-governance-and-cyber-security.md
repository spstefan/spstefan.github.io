---
title: "Data Governance is where Cybersecurity was Ten Years Ago"
summary: "Data Governance has a lot to learn from the field of Cybersecurity in gaining traction and buy-in."
---

When I entered 'IT' almost a decade ago, one of the first things we learned in a cybersecurity course (and witnessed first hand) was that a substantial part of cybersecurity work included begging for buy-in from management. It was a department that bled money off the bottom line, and was accordingly seen as something nice-to-have, but not a core business driver. Additionally, it was seen as a bureacratic and controlling function. I've noticed that picture has changed somewhat in the last decade. Companies are hiring for security more than before[^1], and I've increasingly heard non-technical people speak about it at conferences and podcasts. I also notice how its cultural appeal has evolved into something more respected and sought after amongst a broader group of people. 

Seeing these developments, I'm curious as to how it all happened, because I see data governance sitting in the exact spot that cybersecurity used to occupy a decade ago. 

## How Cybersecurity Evolved

A few things moved cybersecurity out of that pit. First, an increasing number of breaches made major headlines (SolarWinds, Equifax, Vastaamo), which put a number on the losses. Regulation helped too, GDPR especially, once the fines started making headlines. The field changed its language accordingly, and started speaking 'the business' in terms of "here is what we own, and what we lose if it is compromised". By speaking the language of executives, cybersecurity specialists were heard and earned themselves a seat at the table. 

Second, the field went through a cultural rebranding. I remember a time when cybersecurity was synonymous with geeked-out hooded techies and dank server rooms. Lately however, the field has grown more heterogenous, including more women and a wider breadth in professional backgrounds. The conversation around security moved away from technical jargon towards a more socio-technical understanding. Security was increasingly becoming an approachable skill amongst the business. Instead of belonging to the domain of "IT", the regular accountant began pausing before clicking links in his email. All this contributed to disarming the perceived barriers to entry, and as the field gained traction it became even more sought after to 'know' cybersecurity. 

## What Data Governance can Learn from Cybersecurity

Data governance doesn't have the benefit of public breaches for publicity gains, but it can take credit where credit is due. In the coming years there will be a number of success stories related to AI initatives who will inevitably acknowledge the data foundation that got them there. People working with data governance have to be vocal and articulate their role in these projects. 

The impact cybersecurity's cultural rebranding is often overlooked by data people. Who wants to be a Data Steward (read: data janitor) next to roles such as Data Engineer/Scientist/Manager? People joke about rebranding data governance work as "data readiness for AI" so executives will listen, but such language and branding actually has meaningful impact on what ultimately gets decided. 

Leaning into AI is the biggest opportunity for the field of data governance and data management. Who decides the semantics that the machine will rely on when running autonomously? Where do these issues get escalated? If an AI does not have an ontology to base its decisions on, it will confidently make one on the fly. Data governance must be the first thing people think about when asking these questions, and for that to happen, it has to make itself visible to the everyday worker in the organisation.
